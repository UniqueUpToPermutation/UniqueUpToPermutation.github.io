Online Basis Refinement and Compression for Reduced-Order Models

Philip Etter - 06 Oct 2021

Introduction

Model-Order Reduction is a powerful tool for reducing runtime computation for high-fidelity physics models. Fundamentally, at a basic level, all model reduction algorithms are split into two phases as shown in fig (1):

  1. Offline: In the offline phase, one samples training data from a distribution \(\mathcal{D}_\text{train}\) of solutions to an underlying system of parameterized partial differential equations. For example, one might imagine that one is trying to solve for the drag force over an airplane in flight - where the geometry of the airplane is the parameter space and the small tolerances in manufacturing the airplane represent the randomness in distribution \( \mathcal{D}_\text{train} \). The base assumption model reduction techniques make is that in a region of interest \( \Omega \) in the parameter space, the corresponding solutions have an approximately low-dimensional structure. One can construct a reduced model that captures this low-dimensional structure. We denote a reduced model as \( D_\theta(\mathbf{z}) \), where \( D_\theta(\mathbf{z}) \) maps low-dimensional codes \( \mathbf{z} \) to high-dimensional solutions and is parameterized by \(\theta\). One trains the reduced model by fitting to the training data sampled from \( \mathcal{D}_\text{train} \):

    $$ \theta^* = \underset{\theta}{\text{argmin}} \, \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{D}_\text{train}} \min_{\mathbf{z}} \| D_\theta(\mathbf{z}) - \mathbf{x} \|^2 \,.$$
    Note that when the reduced model is linear with dimension \( k \), solving this optimization problem is very efficient, as the solution is given by the first \( k \) left singular vectors of the matrix whose columns are formed from samples from \( \mathbf{x} \).
  2. Online: Once we have our reduced model, we want to project our differential equations into the reduced subspace spanned by the reduced model. There are many ways of doing this, but a good basline is to use a Galerkin projection, i.e., instead of solving

    $$ \mathbf{r}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}\,,$$
    where \( \mathbf{r} \) denotes the residual of our equations, we instead solve

    $$ J_\theta(\mathbf{z})^T \mathbf{r}(D_\theta(\mathbf{z})) = \mathbf{0} \,,$$
    for the latent codes \( \mathbf{z}\). Above \( J_\theta(\mathbf{z}) \) stands for the Jacobian of \( D_\theta(\mathbf{z}) \). Obviously, the second system of equations has far fewer degrees of freedom, and can be solved quite efficiently using subsampling techniques that we will not describe here.

The Problem of Non-Stationarity

In theory, we expect that when we deploy the reduced model in the online phase, that the distribution we deploy on, \( \mathcal{D}_\text{test} \) matches the training distribution \( \mathcal{D}_\text{train} \). However, in practice, this might not always be the case. Often, designing \( \mathcal{D}_\text{train} \) to capture all the possibilities that one might encounter when the reduced model is actually deployed is a quite challenging problem. This is especially true for highly nonlinear problems and rare events - after all, if we don't see something during training, there is little hope that we will be able to recover it during deployment.

In addition to this, there are also problems where the underlying distribution of interest may change over time, where the ability of a reduced model to capture new features it hasn't seen during training is very unlikely.

This fact is a major drawback of model reduction; in fact, one might say that it stands as one of the biggest obstacles preventing the more widespread adoption of these techniques by the scientific community. Naturally, if one uses using computational modelling to build a bridge or engineer an airplane, one wants some amount of gaurantee that the modelling results are accurate. The obvious question becomes:

How do we efficiently ensure that our reduced model will provide accurate results regardless of possible non-stationarity in the problem distribution? (i.e., how can we ensure efficient generalization?)

One approach that practioners sometimes use is to periodically check the full residual to see if the result of the reduced model is accurate. When inaccuracy is detected, one can then fall back to the full model. However, while this approach may gaurantee an accurate solution, it comes at a step cost as the full model is not usually computationally feasible in the online setting.

Alternatively, one might have a series of reduced models of different dimensions, swapping from lower to higher dimension when the reduced model solution becomes inaccurate. However, this approach still suffers from the same underlying issue: the family of models will have to be trained on \( \mathcal{D}_\text{train} \), so there is no reason to believe that any of these reduced models will capture the true solution unless the dimension is comparable to the full model. Obviously, this is not acceptable in terms of performance.

Our Propsed Approach

Our paper provides a comfortable middle ground between an inaccurate reduced model on one hand and an intractible full model on the other. In fact, one could think of the crux of our approach as dynamic model interpolation between the two extremes, with the goal of interpolating only insofar as we can capture the true solution with minimal overhead. For now, we focus only on linear reduced models in this work.

The algorithm we propose is based on two foundational pillars:

Vector Space Sieving for Model Refinement

The first pillar is a novel technique we call vector space sieving and their corresponding data structure refinement trees. This pillar is covered in detail in section (5) of the paper. A refinement tree is a recursive decomposition of a vector space into orthogonal subspaces. At the finest level of the decomposition, every subspace has dimension one and is spanned by a single basis vector. One can construct refinement treesin two steps.

  1. First specifying the orthogonal basis vectors that should span the one dimensional spaces at the bottom of the tree.
  2. Then using recursive k-means clustering to group subspaces into a tree structure by how correlated/anti-correlated the training data is in these subspaces.

Once we have a refinement tree, we refine our inaccurate reduced model by using vector space sieving. This works as follows: for a vector \( \phi \) living in a Hilbert space \( \mathcal{V} \) and an orthogonal decomposition \(\mathcal{V} = \mathcal{U}_1 \oplus \mathcal{U}_2 \oplus ... \oplus \mathcal{U}_n \), we can split \( \phi \) into orthogonal projections onto each of subspaces \( \mathcal{U}_i \),

$$ \phi = \text{proj}_{\mathcal{U}_1}(\phi) + ... + \text{proj}_{\mathcal{U}_n}(\phi) $$

Afterwards, we replace the basis vector \( \phi \) in our model by the collection of vectors \( \{ \text{proj}_{\mathcal{U}_1}(\phi), ..., \text{proj}_{\mathcal{U}_n}(\phi)  \} \). This strictly increases the expressiveness of the model, since any previous state represented by our previous basis can always be represented by our new basis.

Whenever a reduced model is detected to be inaccurate, refinement trees provide plenty of cadidate splits of the above form. To decide which split to make we rely on dual-weight residual error indicators, a heuristic technique that lets us approximate the reduction in error from making a certain split. Our refinement algorithm opperates by finding the best split in the refinement tree for all current basis vectors according to this heuristic. Afterwards, the basis vector is refined by sieving. This process is repeated until we reach a certain level of tolerance.

Fast Kernel Assembly and PCA for Model Compression

The second pillar of our algorithm is a mechanism to reduce the dimensionality of a reduced model that has been refined via vector space sieving. Naturally, the simplest thing one might do to control dimension is the periodically reset the refined reduced model back to its original unrefined form. But in doing this, one sacrifices potentially important new data that was not present in the training data. The question therefore arises:

How can we reduce the dimension of a refined reduced model without sacrificing expressivity?

Our proposed solution is to perform a kernel PCA on the solutions that the refined reduced model has produced online. This allows us to integrate new data without ever reverting to the full model. Moreover, using the kernel trick, one does not even need to perform any calculations in the full-dimensional space.

We spend section (7) of the paper on the details of this process. The entire computation, including the assembly of the kernel can be done without ever performing any computations that scale with the full dimension.

Conclusion

In brief, our paper presents a mechanism for efficiently ensuring accuracy in linear reduced models. Our experiments in section (8) of the paper show that our framework can gaurantee accurate results even when provided with an initial model that is massively inaccurate. For example, on our FitzHugh–Nagumo benchmark, our algorithm achieves ~1% relative error with an average basis dimension of around 20 on a problem of dimension 1024. The original error of the reduced model was ~500%. We think that this and our other benchmarks clearly show that our method allows for accurate, efficient reduced models even in conditions of non-stationarity.
